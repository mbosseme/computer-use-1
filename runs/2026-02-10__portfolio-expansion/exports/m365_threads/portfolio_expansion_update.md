Hey Bill,

Good afternoon! We wanted to share a quick update on the analytical groundwork we've been building for the Portfolio Expansion initiative.

What we did
We conducted a detailed analysis of invoice data (ERP + Remitra) to establish a reliable benchmark for how a typical health system’s non-labor purchasing distributes across service lines (clinical, non-clinical, pharmacy, and food). While the invoice data is not sufficiently granular for product- or category-level analysis (i.e., on- vs. off-contract), it is detailed enough to establish a reliable service line mix.

What this enables
1. Expected volume targets by service line across the full GPO membership
By applying the service line mix to Premier's ~1,775 core GPO hospitals (224K staffed beds), we estimate $122-132B in total non-labor purchasing:
$52-57B in clinical
$50-54B in non-clinical
$18-19B in pharmacy
$2B in food
These benchmarks also establish a reference point for when we pivot to more detailed data sources for future on-/off-/non-contract analysis, helping us identify service line gaps

2. Extension of Supply Analytics coverage to non-clinical
We identified 100 health systems with comprehensive reporting across both clinical and non-clinical spend (the member-submitted and extracted PO/invoice blend)
This allows us to extend existing clinical contract coverage analysis to non-clinical categories immediately (i.e., Brian Hall's team's visualizations), without additional data sourcing
Because this subset represents ~80% of observed GPO clinical volume, we can extrapolate findings to the full membership when we need to estimate, for example, how much additional GPO volume could be brought under contract by expanding coverage across these two service lines (and, what that means in terms of admin fees & growth opportunities)

Note: This analysis focuses on health system and hospital purchasing (the spend flowing through ERPs). The non-acute / alternate site market (retail pharmacy, specialty, etc.) sits outside this framework and would require a separate approach, which we can address in a future phase.

Next steps
Upon receipt of the non-clinical "catch-all" category breakdowns (i.e., splitting IT into more discreet categories), we will begin decomposing subcategories to size on-/off-/non-contract opportunity within the non-clinical space. These breakdowns are expected to be delivered by tomorrow.

Please let us know if there are any questions!

By applying the service line mix to Premier's ~1,775 core GPO hospitals (224K staffed beds), we estimate $122-132B in total non-labor purchasing:$52-57B in clinical$50-54B in non-clinical$18-19B in pharmacy$2B in foodThese benchmarks also establish a reference point for when we pivot to more detailed data sources for future on-/off-/non-contract analysis, helping us identify service line gaps 

2. Extension of Supply Analytics coverage to non-clinical

We identified 100 health systems with comprehensive reporting across both clinical and non-clinical spend (the member-submitted and extracted PO/invoice blend)This allows us to extend existing clinical contract coverage analysis to non-clinical categories immediately (i.e., Brian Hall's team's visualizations), without additional data sourcingBecause this subset represents ~80% of observed GPO clinical volume, we can extrapolate findings to the full membership when we need to estimate, for example, how much additional GPO volume could be brought under contract by expanding coverage across these two service lines (and, what that means in terms of admin fees & growth opportunities) 

Note: This analysis focuses on health system and hospital purchasing (the spend flowing through ERPs). The non-acute / alternate site market (retail pharmacy, specialty, etc.) sits outside this framework and would require a separate approach, which we can address in a future phase.

What we did

We conducted a detailed analysis of invoice data (ERP + Remitra) to establish a reliable benchmark for how a typical health system’s non-labor purchasing distributes across service lines (clinical, non-clinical, pharmacy, and food). While the invoice data is not sufficiently granular for product- or category-level analysis (i.e., on- vs. off-contract), it is detailed enough to establish a reliable service line mix.

 

What this enables

1. Expected volume targets by service line across the full GPO membership

By applying the service line mix to Premier's ~1,775 core GPO hospitals (224K staffed beds), we estimate $122-132B in total non-labor purchasing:$52-57B in clinical$50-54B in non-clinical$18-19B in pharmacy$2B in foodThese benchmarks also establish a reference point for when we pivot to more detailed data sources for future on-/off-/non-contract analysis, helping us identify service line gaps 

2. Extension of Supply Analytics coverage to non-clinical

We identified 100 health systems with comprehensive reporting across both clinical and non-clinical spend (the member-submitted and extracted PO/invoice blend)This allows us to extend existing clinical contract coverage analysis to non-clinical categories immediately (i.e., Brian Hall's team's visualizations), without additional data sourcingBecause this subset represents ~80% of observed GPO clinical volume, we can extrapolate findings to the full membership when we need to estimate, for example, how much additional GPO volume could be brought under contract by expanding coverage across these two service lines (and, what that means in terms of admin fees & growth opportunities) 

Note: This analysis focuses on health system and hospital purchasing (the spend flowing through ERPs). The non-acute / alternate site market (retail pharmacy, specialty, etc.) sits outside this framework and would require a separate approach, which we can address in a future phase.

 

Next steps

Upon receipt of the non-clinical "catch-all" category breakdowns (i.e., splitting IT into more discreet categories), we will begin decomposing subcategories to size on-/off-/non-contract opportunity within the non-clinical space. These breakdowns are expected to be delivered by tomorrow.
